Paper Outline: How Mixture-of-Experts Changes Computation in Small Transformers
================================================================================

1. Introduction (~2 pages)
--------------------------
- Transformers' success, importance of understanding architectural choices
- Mechanistic interpretability on algorithmic tasks (Nanda, Quirke) — but only dense architectures studied
- MoE is increasingly common (Mixtral, Switch) but mechanistically understudied
- Our guiding question: How does FFN architecture affect what attention learns?
- Four variants (FFN, GLU, MoE, MoE-GLU), three tasks (modular addition, add-7, histogram)
- Contributions:
  1. MoE redistributes computation from FFN to attention
  2. MoE accelerates grokking via regularization-routing interaction
  3. GLU hides internal representations from activation probes
  4. MoE-GLU experts partially specialize by operation type

2. Background and Notation (~1.5 pages)
----------------------------------------
- 2.1 One-layer transformer: embedding, positional encoding, attention, FFN, output
  - Math for attention: Q, K, V projections, softmax, multi-head
- 2.2 FFN variants:
  - FFN: W_down(activation(W_up(x)))
  - GLU: W_down(activation(W_gate(x)) * W_up(x))
  - MoE: router p(x) = softmax(W_r * x), top-k selection, dispatch to expert FFNs, aux loss
  - MoE-GLU: same routing, GLU experts
- 2.3 Parameter matching across variants (table of param counts)

3. Experimental Setup (~1 page)
--------------------------------
- 3.1 Tasks:
  - Modular addition: (a+b) mod 113, token format, 30/70 split, full-batch, 40k epochs
  - Add-7: digit-by-digit x+7, reversed order, carry operations (+7/+1/+0), online batches, 10k steps
  - Histogram: count token frequencies, T=32, L=10, backward sampling, 500 epochs
- 3.2 Training: AdamW, hyperparameters per task (table)
- 3.3 Evaluation: exact-match accuracy, multi-seed (5 seeds), error bars
- 3.4 Analysis methods: component ablation (zero attention/FFN output), activation patching,
  linear probes, routing MI, expert ablation, Fourier analysis

4. Results (~8 pages)
----------------------

4.1 MoE Redistributes Computation from FFN to Attention (~2.5 pages)
  - Component ablation on add-7 (no norm): FFN 9.5% without FFN, MoE 55% (Fig 4)
  - Per-position analysis: MoE handles easy positions through attention, reserves experts
    for hard positions (Fig 10)
  - Same pattern on modular addition but weaker: MoE 16.5% vs FFN 1.5% without FFN (Fig 8)
  - Tradeoff: MoE becomes attention-dependent — worse than FFN when attention is ablated
  - Attention pattern analysis: MoE develops cleaner head specialization (digit-copying head,
    self-attention head, context head) vs FFN's messier heads (Fig 11)
  - Mechanistic explanation: routing bottleneck forces attention to develop structured circuits
  - Activation patching confirms causal role: patching attention flips predictions more than
    patching FFN across all variants (Fig 12). FFN has strongest attention-causal signal (0.59
    at tens) because attention is its only cross-position path. MoE-GLU shows highest FFN
    flip rate (0.62 at hundreds), consistent with expert specialization making FFN causally relevant.
  - Histogram results confirm the pattern: MoE 12.5% vs FFN 11.8% without FFN — small but
    directionally consistent. Effect scales with task simplicity (add-7 +46pp, modadd +15pp,
    histogram +0.7pp). Validated across three tasks.
  - Head-specific ablation (sorted by function): MoE's most critical head causes 72% +7 drop
    vs 53% for FFN. Functional specialization is real but head indices swap across seeds.
  - DLA cross-task comparison: ModAdd FFN dominates 88-99% of logits (even MoE only 11% attn),
    Add-7 MoE gets 64% from attention. Redistribution scales with task simplicity.
  - ModAdd attention patterns: all variants identical (~50/50 a/b at = position). No pressure
    for specialization when task requires both components equally.
  - Connection to Glorot et al.: MoE shifts toward relation-based (attention) over
    inventory-based (FFN) computation, but only when the task permits it
  - Connection to Nanda et al.: Modular addition requires discrete Fourier transforms in FFN
    (inherently nonlinear, attention can't compute it). This is why redistribution fails on
    modadd — the core computation exceeds attention's expressive capacity. Redistribution
    scales with how much of the task is within attention's reach.

4.2 MoE Accelerates Grokking via Regularization-Routing Interaction (~2.5 pages)
  - MoE groks 2-3x faster on modular addition, 5/5 seeds vs 3/5 (Fig 1)
  - Regularization baselines: dropout=0.3 is even faster (2.1k vs 8.7k), so it's partly
    regularization (Fig 2)
  - E=1 control: has aux loss but doesn't help — routing to multiple experts matters (Fig 3)
  - Top-k: top-2 kills grokking (0/5) — hard routing essential (Fig A2)
  - Width scaling: advantage grows 1.1x to 2.7x from d=64 to d=256 (Fig 7)
  - Sweet spot at E=2-4 experts, too many degrades

4.3 GLU Hides Internal Structure from Probes (~1.5 pages)
  - Fourier concentration: FFN 0.44, MoE 0.47, GLU 0.07, MoE-GLU 0.18 (Fig 6)
  - Fourier structure over training: FFN neurons develop clean frequency responses,
    GLU neurons don't (Fig 9)
  - GLU doesn't improve task performance — same accuracy, just hides representations
  - Explanation: gate and up projection split information, neither alone reveals the full picture
  - Implication: activation-based interpretability may underestimate GLU models

4.4 Expert Specialization by Operation Type (~1.5 pages)
  - Routing MI: MoE-GLU 0.28 normalized, MoE 0.26 (add-7, no norm)
  - Best case (MoE-GLU seed 42): expert 2 gets 100% of +7, expert 1 gets 92% of +1 (Fig 5)
  - Expert ablation confirms differential impact by operation (Fig 5, right panel)
  - Per-seed variability: specialization is consistent in direction but variable in degree (Fig A4)
  - On modular addition: no specialization — all experts use same Fourier frequencies
  - Specialization emerges on tasks with discrete operation types, not continuous structure

5. Related Work (~1.5 pages)
-----------------------------
- Mechanistic interpretability of arithmetic: Nanda et al. (2023) on grokking and Fourier
  circuits, Quirke & Barez (2024) on addition circuits and parallel digit streams,
  Quirke et al. (2025) on carry/borrow circuits
- Attention vs FFN interplay: Glorot et al. (2025) on counting — relation-based (attention)
  vs inventory-based (FFN), Yuan et al. (2025) on random attention sufficiency
- Mixture of Experts: Shazeer et al. (2017) original MoE, Fedus et al. (2022) Switch
  Transformer, Jiang et al. (2024) Mixtral. MoE interpretability: "A Closer Look into MoE"
  on expert specialization at scale, MONET on monosemantic experts
- GLU and gated architectures: Dauphin et al. (2017) original GLU, Shazeer (2020) SwiGLU,
  Heimersheim & Nanda on bilinear interpretability

6. Discussion & Conclusion (~1 page)
--------------------------------------
- Summary of four findings
- Limitations:
  - 1-layer transformers on toy tasks — unclear if findings transfer to deeper models
  - Add-7 with 3 digits is very easy — all variants solve it, differences only visible under ablation
  - Expert specialization is seed-dependent
  - Dropout baseline complicates the "MoE is special" narrative
- Key takeaway: MoE doesn't just add parameters — it fundamentally changes how computation
  distributes between attention and FFN. Practical implications for interpretability of
  MoE-based LLMs.
- Future work: Deeper models, harder tasks (more digits), theoretical analysis of why routing
  acts as regularization, extending to larger-scale MoE models
